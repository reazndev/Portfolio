Dajia.lol

              
                
What it is

                
Dajia.lol is my take on the whole profile card thing. Yeah, there are tons of these already, but I had a specific problem - I've been tracking everything I listen to with Last.fm since 2021, and there was literally nowhere to show that data in a way that actually looked good.

                
So I built a website where you can show off your music taste alongside all your other links and stuff. It's basically what would happen if a bio link site and your Spotify stats had a baby.

                
Why I made this

                
The whole idea started because Last.fm tracks everything you listen to (Spotify, Apple Music, whatever) - think Spotify Wrapped but every day and way better. But then what? The LastFM website looks horrendous, straight like out of 2005 so the data just sits there without the visuals it deserves. I wanted somewhere to actually display my listening history, top tracks, top artists, all that good stuff, without it looking like a boring spreadsheet.

                
Plus, most link-in-bio sites are pretty bland. They all look the same and don't really show your personality. I wanted something that actually felt like  me .

                
What it does

                
Music stuff (the main thing)

                
    
                
  • Shows your Last.fm data in ways that actually look cool
    • 
                
  • Real-time "currently listening to" widget
    • 
                
  • Top tracks and artists for different time periods (last week, month, year, all time)
    • 
                
  • Your full listening history with nice visuals
    • 
                
  • Basically turns your music taste into something worth showing off
    • 
                

                
Other integrations

                
    
                
  • Discord integration so people can see what you're up to
    • 
                
  • Custom boxes that show more than just the link - they display cool stats like amount of repos, followers etc (GitHub repos, YouTube channels, SoundCloud, countdown timers)
    • 
                
  • Up to 30 different platform links because who wants to send each of their 15 social media accounts with its own link?
    • 
                

                
The whole package

                
Instead of sending people a boring Linktree link, you get one place that actually shows your personality through your music, activity, and everything else.

                
Building it (aka my coding journey)

                
This turned into way more than I expected. What started as "I'll just make a quick site to show my Last.fm data" became months of work and hundreds of hours of coding, designing, scrapping everything, and starting over.

                
The technical mess I created

                
I ended up building four different things:

                
Frontend - React.js (my first real React project, which explains why it took forever)

                
Last.fm data handler - Built this in Rust because why not make it harder for myself

                
Main backend - JavaScript handling the website stuff and database

                
Discord bot - Also JavaScript, grabs your Discord status and throws it in the database

                
The React learning curve from hell

                
This was my first time really using React beyond the obligatory todo app tutorial. I probably rewrote the entire frontend architecture three times because I'd discover hooks, or context, or the fact that putting everything in useState was like storing your entire life in your wallet.

                
The first version was a disaster of prop drilling that went so deep I needed GPS to find where I was passing data. useEffect dependencies were causing infinite re-render loops that made my laptop sound like it was trying to achieve flight.

                
Then I discovered React optimization and went completely overboard. Suddenly everything was wrapped in React.memo, useMemo, and useCallback like I was optimizing for NASA's next Mars mission. I was memoizing components that rendered once a month and caching calculations that took 0.003 milliseconds.

                
The breaking point came when I tried to manage form state manually and ended up with a component that was 400 lines of useState declarations.

                
Database architecture (because I love overengineering)

                
Instead of being a normal person and cramming everything into one table, I decided to build a "properly normalized" database structure. This sounded professional until I realized I now needed JOIN queries just to figure out what color someone wanted their buttons to be.

                
The custom_boxes table is where things got really out of hand. Each box stores metadata about external links - GitHub repos, YouTube channels, Discord servers. The additional_data column is JSONB because every platform returns different information and I gave up trying to create columns for every possible field.

                
Looking back, this is probably over-engineered for what amounts to "show people's music taste and social links," but at least the queries are fast and it makes me feel like a Real Developer when I write JOIN statements to display someone's favorite color.

                
API integration nightmare

                
Every API has different limits and they all hate you in their own special way. It's like dealing with extremely specific bureaucrats who each have incomprehensible rules about how often you're allowed to ask them questions.

                
 Last.fm : 5 requests per second sounds generous until you realize loading a single user profile requires 47 different API calls
                Spotify : Rate limits that change based on popularity (success punishment!)
                Discord : Actually reasonable limits, which made me suspicious
                GitHub : 5,000 requests/hour, but checking if a repo exists costs the same as getting its entire commit history
                Steam : Documentation written by someone who actively hates developers

                
The caching system

                
Building a caching system sounds straightforward until you realize cache invalidation is one of the two hard problems in computer science. I ended up with more caching layers than a fancy pastry, each solving problems created by the previous layer.

                
Browser localStorage, database caching, smart invalidation - it's like conducting a symphony where every musician is playing at their own tempo. The system assumes Last.fm data older than 5 minutes is stale, Discord data older than 30 seconds is definitely stale, and GitHub data older than an hour might be stale unless it's a weekend.

                
The styling rabbit hole

                
What started as "background: blue" turned into building a comprehensive theming system. Users wanted colors, then fonts, then custom backgrounds, then rounded buttons. Suddenly I'm building UI customization that rivals Photoshop's layer styles.

                
CSS-in-JS seemed smart until I was writing JavaScript to generate CSS to style HTML generated by JavaScript. It's turtles all the way down. I eventually settled on CSS custom properties to maintain my sanity.

                
Custom cursors seemed fun until I discovered browser compatibility hell. Animations turned every transition into coordinating multiple elements like a very patient robot choreographer.

                
The features that almost killed me

                
Custom Boxes sounded simple: fetch metadata from URLs and display nice cards. Every platform has its own special damn way of being difficult. GitHub wants auth sometimes, YouTube's API costs money if you breathe wrong, Steam's docs were written by committee via passive-aggressive post-it notes.

                
 Audio System : Users wanted background music until I discovered browsers actively prevent auto-playing audio. It's like trying to surprise someone who has to explicitly consent to being surprised.

                
 Template System : Let users save/share configurations. Required versioning, permissions, and basically building an app store for profile layouts.

                
The stuff that keeps breaking

                
 CORS Issues : APIs don't work from browsers because "security" > "my app working"
                Mobile Safari : Apple decided web standards are suggestions. Things work everywhere except Safari, which runs on vindictive physics
                API Changes : GitHub randomly deprecates endpoints, Spotify changes auth flows, Last.fm decides artist images need different formats because Thursday
                User Content : Users upload 50MB corrupted images and create usernames with emoji that break URL routing

                
Performance wins

                
    
                
  • Code splitting with Vite (automatic but I optimized the important routes)
    • 
                
  • Image optimization : WebP conversion, responsive sizing, lazy loading for anime wallpapers
    • 
                
  • Database indexing : Turns out CS theory matters - proper indexes made everything fast
    • 
                
  • Virtual scrolling : Only render visible items instead of crashing browsers with 1000+ track lists
    • 
                

                
What actually works

                
After months of building and rebuilding:

                
    
                
  • Sub-second page loads (proper caching works)
    • 
                
  • Real-time music data that updates when you change songs
    • 
                
  • Mobile-first design that works everywhere
    • 
                
  • Customization system for unique profiles
    • 
                
  • Stable API integrations that handle failures gracefully
    
                
    Architecture that survived :
    
                
    • 
                

                
Frontend: React 18 + Vite
                Database: PostgreSQL (properly normalized)
                Auth: Discord OAuth
                Caching: Multi-layer (browser + database + smart invalidation)

                
The hosting situation (aka why I hate myself)

                
Here's where things get really stupid: this entire over-engineered monstrosity is hosted on a Raspberry Pi 3 that's probably older than some of my users. We're talking 1GB of RAM and 16GB of storage - specifications that were modest when I was still in Kindergarden.

                
This little plastic computer, which costs less than a decent lunch, is somehow serving real-time music data, processing image uploads, managing database connections, and handling API requests for multiple users simultaneously. It's like asking a Honda Civic to tow a yacht - technically possible, but you're gonna hear some concerning noises.

                
The Pi sits in my basement so if it ever catches fire because some user decides to upload a 200MB pictures of his mother I'll know far too late that I have to call the fire department. (You can see the thermals of my "server" if you can even call it that, in the starting section of my website under "server temps")

                
The really absurd part is that it actually works. Users are getting sub-second page loads from a device that has less computing power than the smartphone that my grandma still uses. The secret is aggressive caching, smart resource management, and the fact that most of the heavy lifting happens in users' browsers thanks to React doing its thing client-side.

                
Sometimes I lie awake at night wondering what would happen if this thing actually got popular. The Pi would probably just catch fire and take my entire house with it. But until then, it's kind of amazing that you can build something legitimately useful on hardware that costs less than a train ticket from my house to my school.

                
The result

                
It's a website where you can actually show off who you are instead of just having a list of links. Your music taste, what you're doing on Discord, your projects, whatever you want - all in one place that doesn't look like every other generic profile page.

                
Plus I learned a ton building it, even if I wanted to throw my laptop out the window multiple times during the process.

                
Is it going to revolutionize the internet? Probably not. Is it a pretty cool way to show off your digital life? Yeah, I think so.
